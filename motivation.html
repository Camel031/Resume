Motivation Letter — Academia Sinica Research Internship

In my second year of high school, I placed 15th at the NHSPC Regional Programming Contest and walked away feeling less satisfied than I had expected. I had spent months learning algorithms, founded my school's competitive programming team at CLHS, built its training curriculum, and ran winter camps. The rankings improved. But the more time I spent optimizing within the closed world of a judge system, the more I kept asking what, exactly, I was solving. The problems were elegant. The feedback was immediate and clean. And none of it touched anything real. There was also something harder to admit: the gains were flattening. I had done everything right — built the team, ran the camps, worked through the algorithms systematically — and the ceiling was getting closer. Some find that a reason to push harder; I found it a reason to ask whether I was climbing the right wall. That restlessness followed me into university.

At National Taiwan Normal University, I chose a double major in Computer Science and Life Science partly to escape that lane and find problems that were genuinely harder to bound. Life Science offered them — but pure biology moved by intuition and experiment in ways that felt foreign to how I thought. The transition was harder than I had expected: caught between the competitive-programmer identity I was leaving and a field I hadn't yet internalized, my grades in the first two years showed the confusion — dipping as low as 2.09 in a semester. Things started connecting in my third year; my GPAs for the three semesters since have been 3.27, 4.15, and 4.14. What clicked, eventually, was Bioinformatics. The data was noisy, high-dimensional, deeply personal — the kind that sits at the boundary between computation and biology in a way that resists clean formulation. One question kept coming back: what does it mean to extract a useful signal from data that its subject might not want exposed?

That question sharpened in my Information Security Practices for Industries: Attack and Defense course, and more so during the AIS BOMBE red/blue team CTF. On the offensive side, I built a full evasion chain: PPID spoofing, dynamic IAT resolution, XOR + Base64 payload encryption, and decoy process injection to bypass static signatures and behavioral analysis — extracting flags via VirtualQueryEx / ReadProcessMemory and credentials via Living-off-the-Land, all without touching disk. On the defensive side, I co-built an ETW-based behavioral monitor with file I/O and TCP event tracing, and implemented recursive parent-chain attribution to associate spawned processes with malicious ancestors. Simultaneously building an attack and building the detector for it — watching each side expose the blind spots of the other — was the moment security stopped being a subject and became a practice. What stayed was the habit of thinking about systems from both sides of the boundary. It also showed me where my own gaps were, which is part of why I started working toward the OSCP — treating it less as a credential than as a structured framework to systematically audit and extend my offensive depth across areas a single course or CTF doesn't cover.

The question became more concrete when I joined Normal OJ as full-stack Tech Lead and Sandbox subteam lead for NTNU's CS Department — a production online judge system that had been passed down across multiple cohorts. My first priority was stabilizing what was there: hardening the sandbox execution layer, cleaning up the CI/CD pipeline, and coordinating backend, frontend, and cloud deployment across GCP and Cloudflare before extending any of it. Auditing the codebase as I took over, I found real vulnerabilities: an unauthenticated GET /ranking endpoint returning the complete user database — email-derived MD5 hashes, role assignments, submission histories — with no credentials required; and homework management routes whose access control existed only in the frontend. I documented both as formal write-ups. Finding a vulnerability in a CTF is one thing; finding it in code you're responsible for shipping to real users is another. The question shifted from how systems fail to what it would take to build one that doesn't. That led me to think about adding a behavioral authentication layer on top of the sandbox — but any system that authenticates by observing how you move or type has to transmit those signals somewhere, and they're as personal as a fingerprint with no way to issue a new one.

That question led me to homomorphic encryption, and eventually into NTNU's Applied Cryptography and Information Security Laboratory under Prof. Chi Po-Wen (紀博文), where I am working on Privacy-Preserving Continuous Behavioral Authentication via FHE-Accelerated BTF and FIDO2 PRF Extension — an open problem: FIDO and WebAuthn secure the login event but offer no protection against post-login session hijacking. Behavioral biometrics — mouse dynamics, keystroke patterns, touch gestures, and other continuous signals — can fill that gap, but virtually all deployed systems today send those raw signals to the server; a breach permanently compromises every user's behavioral profile. Fully Homomorphic Encryption resolves this by allowing inference directly on ciphertext, returning only a binary result — but FHE ciphertexts are orders of magnitude larger than plaintext, making real-time transmission infeasible without compression. My design addresses this by encrypting behavioral features with Trivium (De Cannière & Preneel; eSTREAM portfolio) before sending — achieving 121× compression — while transciphering into the FHE domain on the server side. The last piece is key custody: by routing decryption through the user's FIDO hardware authenticator, the final step happens inside the secure enclave rather than on any server.

Outside the lab, the same period deepened my connection to the technical community. I've been involved with SITCON — Taiwan's largest student-run technology conference — for over five years, and in 2023–2024 served as Vice Head Coordinator. That meant overseeing 10+ working groups, handling speaker submission review, program coordination, and sponsorship management via GitLab, and getting 1,000+ attendees and 150+ staff through event day without critical failures. I also served as Team Counselor at SITCON Camp 2024, mentoring a hackathon team through full project delivery. Through COSCUP and TWNOG I've stayed connected to the open-source and networking communities.

Beyond research and community work, I have a consistent record of shipping production systems under real conditions. I founded 萬應製作 All-In-One Production and built an SMS (Stage Management System) now managing 1,000+ pieces of equipment across concurrent live productions. The work — programming grandMA2 consoles, designing signal flow and fixture networks, building a Lua plugin for showfile generation and a 3D truck layout simulator for load-in planning, with LLM-assisted prototyping throughout — gave me a rule I carry into every software project: the system has to handle failure, because something always breaks in ways the spec didn't anticipate.

What draws me to Academia Sinica is not a single research direction, but the breadth and rigor of the work happening there. My current interests sit at the intersection of security, privacy, and bioinformatics. I've trained CNNs, fine-tuned open-source models with PyTorch, and have the FHE project ongoing in Prof. Chi's lab, but there's only so far you get working on your own. My longer-term goal is graduate research, and I believe the most direct path there runs through an environment like this one.
